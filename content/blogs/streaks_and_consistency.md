I’ve always been someone who struggled with discipline and consistency.
And from the very beginning, I was fed this idea that consistency is the key — that if you’re consistent enough, compounding will eventually do its magic and you’ll succeed.

> So, to succeed, I have to be consistent, right?
> Well — yes.

Now, when you hear the word **consistency**, what’s the first thing that comes to mind?

*Streaks.*

And this is where things get interesting.

At first glance, streaks and consistency sound like the same thing. But there’s a very fine line between them — and that line makes a world of difference.

### Streaks ≠ Consistency.

So what exactly is a streak, and how is it different?

Let’s oversimplify it.

**A streak is just a way to record the days you showed up. That’s it.**

Go check any habit tracker or platform like GitHub or LeetCode. For them, a streak simply means showing up every single day without breaking the chain. Whether you actually improved, learned something new, or pushed your limits doesn’t matter — only your presence does.

And this is the main crux of the problem.

Streaks don’t care if you’re evolving. They don’t care if today’s effort was better than yesterday’s. They only care about one thing:

**Were you there, or were you not?**

But when we say consistency leads to results, do we really mean *just* marking attendance every day?
Or do we mean gaining even the slightest bit of new understanding, skill, or clarity each day?

Obviously, the latter.

Yet streaks don’t capture that. You could be repeating the same low-effort task daily, learning nothing new, and still maintain a “perfect” streak.

---

Now let’s take a real example — LeetCode.

LeetCode uses streaks to keep users engaged, tapping directly into the dopamine loop under the guise of progress tracking. I won’t say it’s completely pointless — it does track activity. But here’s the catch.

That calendar chart? It’s just a grid of days.

- **Green** means you showed up.
- **Grey** means you didn’t.

That’s it.

No distinction between solving a hard problem after deep thinking and mindlessly submitting a solution just to keep the streak alive.

And ask yourself this: when those green dots start looking insanely satisfying on your profile, won’t a lot of people slowly shift their focus from learning to dot-chasing?

### A Quick Story

Let me share a quick story.

A friend of mine grinds LeetCode. He’s obsessed with streaks — completely hooked on those green squares. I checked his activity over the last two months. Not a single day missed. The entire chart was green.

Sounds impressive, right?

Except there’s one detail.

We had exams for the entire last month.

This is how his graph looked.

![screenshot](/blog_1_images/image.png)

Now, you might say, “So what’s the problem here? He’s just more dedicated and consistent.”
And honestly, I believed that too — until I noticed something odd.

I asked him, *“How did you manage to keep this up during exams? And why are your total submissions almost twice the number of questions you’ve actually solved?”*

His answer was simple:

> “During exams I obviously didn’t have time to solve new questions. And I didn’t want my streak to end, so I just re-submitted questions I had already solved.”

At this point, you probably already see the problem — but let me spell it out clearly.

For an entire month, he didn’t care about:

- learning a new concept
- discovering a new approach
- struggling, brainstorming, or improving his problem-solving ability

All he cared about was **not letting the streak break.**
Even if that meant repeating the same set of questions again and again.

---

### The Real Problem With Streaks

And this is the real problem with streaks.

Streaks don’t make you addicted to learning or excelling —
**they make you addicted to showing up.**

They shift your focus from growth to optics. From progress to appearance. From learning to green-dot chasing.

But what’s the point of showing up if you’re not even slightly better than yesterday?

I know this because I’ve been a victim of this myself.

I started the `#100DaysOfCode` challenge on X. The first few days were genuinely solid. I was learning, experimenting, and pushing myself. But slowly, things changed.

I started chasing breadth instead of depth.
I began consuming and posting countless jargon-heavy terms just because they looked good in a daily update.

The goal was simple:
*learn something new or polish existing skills every day.*

But very soon, the goal silently shifted to something else:
*“Do whatever it takes to avoid a blank day on X.”*

Sure, you could argue that at least I showed up every day.
But what’s the use of showing up if, after all that effort, I’m still the same person I was yesterday?

So remember this:

### Streaks are a myth.

They’re great at keeping you hooked, but terrible at ensuring real growth. They push you to chase numbers instead of mastery — and that trade-off is never worth it.

- Be obsessed with learning.
- Be obsessed with the process.

No one is going to reward you for a profile filled with green dots. At best, it’ll only satisfy you.

What actually matters is this:

1. Did you learn something new today?
2. Did you refine an existing skill?
3. Are you even a little better than yesterday?

If yes — **you’re consistent.**
Even without a streak.
